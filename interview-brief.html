INTERVIEW THESIS BRIEF

OPX Chief of Staff
Westinghouse

A discussion document for testing one central idea: the Chief of Staff can help the business scale by keeping commitments, constraints, decisions, and learning inside one executive operating envelope.

1. Mandate hypothesis

Nominal role: OPX Chief of Staff, Cranberry Township.

Actual mandate hypothesis: Turn a broad, safety-critical operating business into a more coherent executive system—one that converts customer commitments, delivery risk, capacity constraints, operating signals, and cross-functional dependencies into faster decisions and reliable follow-through without adding unnecessary bureaucracy.

Evidence boundary: the public job URL identifies the title and location, but the full posting text was not reliably retrievable through public indexing during research. Specific internal-remit recommendations here are therefore hypotheses for discovery.

2. Company moment

Fleet-scale ambition

Westinghouse announced a DOE financing partnership tied to long-lead items for up to 10 AP1000 units and said advance purchase could accelerate deployment by up to three years.

Repeatability pressure

The company is advancing a standard AP1000 reference plant for U.S. fleet-scale deployment, explicitly tying standardization to licensing and delivery predictability.

Operating-plant breadth

The public portfolio spans engineering, outages, parts, digital optimization, I&C, plant services, staffing, and long-term operations.

Execution credibility

External reporting frames the opportunity alongside cost, schedule, component-quality, supply-chain, and workforce execution risks that the nuclear sector must continue to manage.

3. Operating tensions

Growth vs. execution discipline

More opportunity increases the cost of weak prioritization, hidden dependencies, and late escalation.

Speed vs. nuclear-grade governance

Decision latency matters, but speed cannot blur safety, quality, regulatory, or technical authority.

Portfolio breadth vs. one executive truth

Different service lines need local judgment while leaders need a coherent view of commitments and constraints.

Customer urgency vs. capacity planning

Field windows and customer commitments compete with scarce technical, supply-chain, and execution capacity.

Global scale vs. local accountability

Standardize visibility and thresholds without centralizing decisions best made close to the work.

4. Candidate thesis: Executive Operating Envelope

Make execution legible before it becomes urgent. Build a four-layer executive system:

Commitment Map

Outcome · date · customer · owner · confidence · dependencies.

Constraint Ledger

Capacity · parts · engineering · field window · quality/regulatory gates · aging.

Decision Architecture

Threshold · owner · decision right · escalation route · aging · closure.

Learning Registry

Recurring cause · reusable fix · standard work · planning assumption · adoption.

The Chief of Staff is not the owner of every decision. The role creates the visibility and governance that make decision ownership clearer.

5. Decision architecture to test

  1. Where does executive attention go now?
  2. What deserves intervention versus local resolution?
  3. Where should scarce expertise be allocated?
  4. Which commitment is losing confidence?
  5. What recurring failure mode needs a system fix?
  6. What work or reporting should stop?

8. Hard objection

“You have not spent your career inside a nuclear OEM or utility.”

Response: Correct. I would not present adjacent experience as direct nuclear experience. My value proposition is high-reliability operating discipline, cross-functional visibility, decision architecture, quality and root-cause mechanisms, regulated execution, and adoption. Nuclear-specific authority remains with nuclear experts; the Chief of Staff mechanism should make their signals more visible and decisions easier to route.

10. Executive interview questions

  1. Where does the leadership team spend disproportionate time reconciling different versions of reality?
  2. Which decisions are routinely escalated too late—and which are escalated too often?
  3. Where do customer urgency and internal capacity collide most often?
  4. What execution risk worries the OPX leader that current dashboards do not reveal early enough?
  5. What must remain local because judgment is closest to the work—and what benefits from standardization?
